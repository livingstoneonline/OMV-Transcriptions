Letter to J.J. Freeman. 23 May 1850

Author(s) & contributor(s): James Read, Jr.

Place(s) of creation: Philipton

Repository: University of London. School of Oriental and African Studies (London, United Kingdom)

Shelfmark / Identifier: CWML, Incoming correspondence, 25/3/D

Digital edition & date: One More Voice, an imprint of Livingstone Online, 2020

Critical editing & encoding: Heather F. Ball, Mary Borgo Ton, Adrian S. Wisnicki

Note: This historical document, published in unabridged form, reflects the cultural distortions and prejudices of its time and may contain material that will upset or distress some readers.

Please turn your mobile device to landscape or widen your browser window for optimal viewing of this archival document.

0001

                                                Philipton
                                                    May 23 1850




5

My dear Mr Freeman


                    Owing to the detentions of
the past by the rains and my absence into
Caffreland for the recovery of Mrs Read,
and one of our childrens healths I could
10not reply to yours of the 4 ultimo till now.


1[.] I shall take the earliest opportunity of communicating
with Mr Ulbrecht about his son.


2. I have not had any recent information
about Madaars country - however I dont hear
15a word now about the task - On Campbell
shaking his tardant over Lin H by the
publication of your letter may for
0002
the present farther proceedings –
I met Mr Caldewood yesterday & he
told me that the Boars have been causing
a disturbance among the Tambookies, that is
5they have threatened to drive them out
of their country & that [      ] he had advised
the [      ] to send a force to apprehend
the ringleaders away the Boars - but
that Lin H thinks from it - It is to be
10bourne in mind that He first promised
the Boers that he would parcel out
Tamkaolund among them - I think
Lin A spoke about it to you[.]


3. "I know one you likely to be offended by the
15constitution "9" If justice is done there & is if
all who have property to the amount of 50 £
be fit for [      ] then will be nearly 7 or [ ]8 as he
0003
have the right of duking as nearly all the
cups are valued by the Road Board at
from 50 to 70 £ –     The diamond should
be [      ] immediately as then ostensible reason
5for not building good houses is for
want of that & the ^ want of security & sense of security
of life and property - The Natives look on
the present peace as a hushed volcano = therefore
they are disinclined to build at least for the
10present -


4. The holder of lands at Kat River may sell
and transfer these lands - & therefore it is very
likely in a shout the lands may fall into the
hands of others —


15

5[.] The only thing that can put off this is that the
English Prolution Society buy up all vacant
cufs. & sell them again to other industrious
Natives - This There is no risk in such
0004
a plan. The old English anti-slavery &
Prolution Societies must not abandon
the Natives —


6. I am not of opinion that the Natives will
5suffer greatly from the Establishment of a
representative assembly - but move from a
federal union -


7 I shall write to Mr [      ] by next
post about the accounts -


10

I intent this newly as an outline of my
views - but I shall write deliberately write in
the conjunction with my father & drink
to the mission Bloanfuld thaut? —


Be so kind my dear Lin as to present
15my kindest reagrds to Mrs Freeman
& your excellent daughters & believe me


                    Yours respectfully & affectionately


                                                James Read
